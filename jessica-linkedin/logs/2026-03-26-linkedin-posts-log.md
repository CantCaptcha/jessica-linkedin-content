**Title:** What I Expected vs. What I Found as a Startup COO
**Author:** Jessica Allen - Transformational Leadership: Building High-Performing Businesses

### Post Content
When I came on as COO of a small company facing growing pains, I expected to find basic ad-hoc processes that needed process diagrams and training. I thought my job was to invest time getting everyone "rowing in the same direction" from a macro level. I was wrong. What I actually found was that the most valuable activity was jumping into the work itself — grabbing individual tasks and making incremental improvements.

Even those small iterative changes send shockwaves through a team. People resist change, even when it's for better. But as we adopted each one, something interesting happened: those task-level improvements started connecting organically, creating a macro change I thought I had to force from the top down.

My critical role as COO isn't about imposing perfect processes. It's about seeing connections across individual tasks — seeing the macro view — and managing the shockwaves of change so the team can adapt. Sometimes the best strategy isn't starting at 30,000 feet. It's in the weeds, improving one task at a time, and letting the big picture emerge.

What's been your experience — macro strategy or incremental execution?

## Post #2 - March 24, 2026 (2 days ago)

### Post Details
**Title:** Green Doesn't Always Mean Good. Red Doesn't Always Mean Alarm.
**Author:** Jessica Allen - Transformational Leadership: Building High-Performing Businesses

### Post Content
As a CIO and now COO, visibility is non-negotiable. But the real question isn't "what are our metrics?" — it's "which metrics actually matter?"

I've spent last ~5 years using EOS and Bloomgrowth to track KPIs across IT performance, operational execution, and business outcomes. The system gives you a weekly cadence of green vs red. Simple. Quantitative.

Except I've walked out of executive rooms wondering:
- "Why doesn't anyone care that this is red?"
- "Why don't I feel good when everything shows as green?"

Here's what I've learned:
1. Transparency is table stakes. You have to do it — at multiple levels of business. No exceptions.
2. Debate metrics with diverse perspectives. The person closest to the KPI is often least able to see it without bias. Get different viewpoints around the table.
3. Iterate until it creates confidence. KPIs aren't easy. Keep refining until everyone agrees it's exposing something meaningful.
4. Industry benchmarks are a starting point, not the final answer. Your company, customers, and economic landscape are unique. Make them yours.
5. Directionally appropriate beats perfect. Be OK with "good enough" if it drives better decisions than not measuring at all.
6. If you have more than 10-15 metrics, you're doing it wrong. I remember Monthly Operating Reviews (affectionately "MORs") at Citi with 80-100 pages just from my view in one business unit. Maybe 20% of the full report. Let's hope someone made sense of it, but I doubt it. It was too complex, too detailed, and too hard to consume. Don't do that.

Metrics are an art, not a science. The skill isn't measuring. It's knowing which indicators are signals vs. noise.

### Post Content (Version 2 - Authentic Voice - FINAL VERSION)

**Building High-Performance Teams Without Breaking Culture**

You hear it everywhere: "Move fast and break things." But here's what no one talks about: when you move that fast without culture, the culture breaks.

Over the last few years, a concept I've come to understand is that culture isn't a poster on the wall. It's not an employee handbook. Culture is what's felt every day — in every decision, every conversation, every way we treat each other. And that feeling either builds trust or erodes it instantly.

I've seen it happen. Companies that scale fast — they hire, they grow, they push — and somewhere along the way, they lose what made them special in the first place. The thing that attracted their best people and kept them engaged through the tough times. Gone.

Because here's the harder truth: Culture has to be intentional. It has to be alive in the environment. You should be able to see your core values in how you hire, how you reward, and yes, even how you make hard decisions. If those values aren't real — if they're just words on a wall but we allow violations every week — then you don't have culture. You have wallpaper.

When I was at P&G, we had this test everyone called the "personality test." Infamous. I hated it. But now, with distance, I understand the intent. They were trying to fit us — to see if we'd "last" in that environment. But a multiple-choice test can't capture heart, or integrity, or whether someone will show up when things are hard. It can't tell you if your culture matches what you say it is.

What I've learned is that building high-performance teams isn't about adding more processes or systems. Those are tools, not solutions. The foundation has to be your people and your values. The question isn't "how do we scale?" It's "how do we scale without losing what makes us who we are?"

I don't have all the answers, and I'm not sure anyone does. But I know this much: whatever "high-performance" means for your team, it has to start with who you already are — not with who you think you should be.

Your culture is what's felt every day. What you write down and say your culture is must match that.

What's your experience — building teams fast while keeping what matters intact?
